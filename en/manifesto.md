# Don't break the machine. Own it.

This week, in *Le Monde*, a collective of writers and artists called for a boycott of AI. I make images with these machines. And that doesn't put me in the opposite camp, the one with the people who promise you that in five years everything will be sorted out, that you just have to accelerate, trust, sign. I stand in the place where you're allowed to be nowhere, between the two. That place, I'm going to try to describe it, because a lot of us occupy it without daring to say so.

I'll start with what I feel. The rest comes from there.

Two years ago, I was spending nights in front of a screen watching a model generate faces. Not to make something pretty. For a film. One night, around three in the morning, an image came out, and I recognized a gesture that was mine. The way I frame a face in three-quarter view, cut a little too short by the edge, the thing I've been doing for ten years without thinking about it. Except this time I hadn't done it. The machine had done it. It was handing me back my own gesture, severed from me, laid on the table like an object held out to me with the words: here, this is yours, buy it. I never forgot that moment. It isn't wonder. It isn't terror either. It's more precise and more unpleasant. The sensation of being doubled from the outside, of seeing what defines me come back to me as a commodity I no longer own.

The anxiety in front of AI gets treated as a weakness. A lack of courage, or a lack of information, something you'd dispel by explaining how transformers work. I don't believe that. The anxiety is a product. It is manufactured, and it is manufactured by two very concrete things.

The first is the freezing. When you're told over and over that it's inevitable, that a steamroller is coming and there's nothing you can do, your body does what every body does in front of a predator too big to fight. It freezes. Joseph Weizenbaum, who had himself built one of the first conversational programs, put it more harshly than anyone: the myth of technological inevitability is "a powerful tranquilizer of the conscience." The tranquilizer doesn't calm you. It puts to sleep the part of you that could act. They sell you the freezing as lucidity.

The second is incomprehension. You use, every day, a thing you know nothing about. You type a prompt, an image comes out, and between the two there's a black hole. Asma Mhalla, in *Technopolitique*, sees this as the main spring of the stupor: a "profound incomprehension of how these systems work," which she proposes to "demystify" in order to "inhibit our primary emotions, fear first of all." Anne Alombert says the same thing differently in *Schizophrénie numérique*: the stupor masks the fact that we don't understand, and the cure is neither to demonize nor to reject, it's to re-credit the public, to give it back the capacity to understand, to break things down, to make.

I hold on to that. I add one thing that neither of them says loudly enough, and which is the real reason the anxiety doesn't dissolve with a technical lesson.

Understanding how it works isn't enough to reassure you, because what you're facing isn't just a poorly explained tool. It's an imagination of a different kind than yours, what Grégory Chatonsky calls artificial imagination. An imagination without a body, without desire, without death. It produces faces without ever having feared growing old, landscapes without ever having been cold. When the image comes out at three in the morning and it's right, it's not because the machine "understands" my film. It's because it recombined millions of gestures that aren't its own. Those gestures come from somewhere. They come from people. Often from dead people. The model is trained on centuries of images, of texts, of framings made by painters, photographers, filmmakers who are no longer here. The dead labor Marx spoke of, the fixed capital that sells you back your own gesture, is not only your own labor objectified. It's the labor of the dead. A statistical necromancy. The machine revives the gestures of the departed and serves them to you as new, and you, the living, find yourself renting the use of what ghosts made for free.

Telling yourself "it's only statistical calculation" calms you no more than "it's inevitable." Both phrases are symmetrical tranquilizers. One puts you to sleep with fatality, the other with contempt. Reducing AI to statistics is a refusal to look at what's strange about it: an imagination that works without a subject. To understand it is not to reduce it. It's to look it in the face, with its share of the spectral.

Now, the two stories you're offered for inhabiting this situation. I refuse them both, and for the same reason: neither gives you back any power.

The first story is the serf's. You're overtaken, AI is going to take your work, and the only thing left to you is to refuse. To unplug. To keep yourself pure, off to the side. This is the story of the boycott. It defends just things, consent, payment, the refusal to let our works be taken without anyone asking. But look closely at what gesture it is: it casts you as a consumer. To boycott is the gesture of someone who decides not to buy. You close the tab, you don't use the tool, and you think you've acted. You haven't touched ownership. The infrastructure still runs, the fiefs still collect, the compute still belongs to the same people. You withdrew, that's all. Mark Fisher called this the safety valve: the system lets you express your refusal precisely because that refusal costs it nothing. You abandon the ground instead of contesting it.

The second story is the spectator's. Relax, enjoy, the technology is neutral, look how beautiful it is, in five years you'll have your studio in your pocket. This is the story of acceleration. It too gives you back no power, because it asks of you the exact opposite of the serf for the same result: don't touch anything, let it happen, trust those who own. The serf withdraws, the spectator applauds. In both cases ownership doesn't budge an inch.

That's where the real dividing line is. Not between those who use AI and those who refuse it. Between those who own it and those who rent it.

The Luddites were put on the trial they'd put me on today: backward-lookers who break the machines. It's false, and the historian E. P. Thompson established it long ago. The Luddites weren't against technology. They smashed specific looms, those of the bosses who lowered wages, and they spared the others. Hobsbawm called it "collective bargaining by riot." Brian Merchant, in *Blood in the Machine*, says it again for our time: their fight was never about the existence of machines, but about who controls them and who pockets what they produce. Real Luddism isn't breaking ChatGPT. It's reorganizing who owns the model and who collects the gains.

Cédric Durand gives this domination a vocabulary I love because it's carnal, medieval, dirty. The fief. The glebe. The robota, the word "robot" comes from, which named the free labor owed to the lord. When you take out a subscription to a model, you're not buying a tool, you're becoming what Varoufakis calls a cloud serf, a serf of the digital glebe. You pay to get in, and once inside you work for free for the lord: each of your prompts, each of your corrections, each of your clicks refines his capital. Varoufakis sums it up in a sentence: "Enter amazon.com and you have exited capitalism." These are no longer markets, they're fiefs. It's no longer profit, it's rent. You till land that isn't yours and you hand over the harvest to the owner.

So, own. Hold your own weights, run the model locally, on your own machine, in your own room. Retrain it on your own images, teach it a style with a hand-made LoRA, open the model, audit it, refuse the censorship the owner inscribed in it for his own interests. Durand has an exact word for this gesture: the allod. The free land, "a terrain belonging only to them, at the frontiers of the known world." Cutting the enclosure downstream. No longer paying the toll on every image. Keeping your own eye instead of subcontracting it.

I do this. And I'm going to tell you why it isn't enough, because I distrust myself when I get triumphant.

First, there's no vantage point from which I'd cleanly dominate the machine. The idea that I "own my eye," that I impose my vision and the tool obeys, is a fantasy of mastery. When I work in the latent space, I don't command, I wait. I wait for what comes. The model offers me things I didn't have in mind, and some of them reconfigure me. I retrain it, yes, but in the meantime it retrains me too, it shifts my taste, it teaches me framings I wouldn't have found alone. There's what Grégory Chatonsky calls a latency: in the end, I don't always know which part of the result comes from me. Stiegler named this double edge the pharmakon, the poison that is at the same time the remedy. Chatonsky, in *Les deux pharmakon*, draws from it the consequence I want to hold to all the way: there's no vantage point from which to cleanly separate good use from bad, to keep the remedy and throw out the poison. The remedy is woven from the poison. Owning your model is a care, and it's also a new dependency, a new way of letting yourself be worked by the machine. I hold it, and it holds me.

Then, my allod runs on the chain I denounce. It has to be said head-on, because it's the strongest argument against everything I'm saying, and it comes from Nastasia Hadjadji, in *Apocalypse Nerds*. Her charge is simple: AI is propped up by an infrastructure of power, chips, rare earths, water, energy, annotators paid a pittance in the countries of the South, and to claim you're reclaiming its use locally is to launder this industry. She's right about the facts. The room where I work heats up because an Nvidia card, etched by TSMC on ASML machines, burns electricity beside me to manufacture my images. The model I retrain was pre-trained somewhere, on water-cooled servers, from corpora that include works used without consent, which rights holders are now contesting in court. And the filters I use were made bearable by people in Madagascar paid between ninety-six and one hundred twenty-six euros a month, by Kenyan moderators paid less than two dollars an hour who come out of it with post-traumatic syndromes. Antonio Casilli documented this click-work. It is, of the entire anti-AI op-ed, the best-established argument. Owning locally the product of an enclosure does not undo the enclosure. My gesture doesn't redeem itself on its own.

That's why I don't say: own your weights and you'll be free. I say: the allod is worth nothing if it stays alone. The means of production, Marx reminded us, is not an object you carry home, it's a social relation. The general intellect, the concept Marx forges to name the accumulated intelligence of humanity that the machine recombines, is collective by nature, and its true reappropriation can only be collective. My isolated free land, if it stops there, is a cabin I build for myself in a corner while the whole forest stays fenced off. Durand and Varoufakis both point to the same way out, and it isn't the isolated land, it's the commons. Open weights, but also open data and open governance. Public compute. And collective bargaining, on the model of what the American screenwriters wrested in 2023 with their strike, which didn't break a single machine and yet bent the industry on numbered clauses. The allod is my gesture of exemplarity. The commons is the politics. One without the other can't stand up.

There remains the question of slop, because it's used every which way and it poisons the whole debate.

Slop isn't the ugly image, nor the machine-made image, nor the vulgar image you'd set against the noble work. If you define slop by taste, you fall right back into Bourdieu's old hierarchy, pure taste against popular taste, the avant-garde against the kitsch that Greenberg was already denouncing in 1939. And this weapon immediately turns against me: my "auteur AI" against the "slop of the masses" is word for word the same structure of distinction, the same cultural capital disguised as aesthetic judgment. If I pull out Bourdieu against the others, he comes back in my face.

Slop, as Hadjadji and Tesquet argue, has only one criterion, and it isn't aesthetic, it's political: the regime of production. An image is slop if it answers to no one, if it's pure extraction with no author, content spat out to fill a feed and capture one second of attention on a phone held on the subway. The only question is: does someone answer for this image? A roundup meme, a racist propaganda image made by machine, is not slop. It's the opposite. Someone wanted it, intended it, signed it right down to its intention. It's auteur propaganda, and it's worse than slop, not better. Don't ask the word "slop" to do two jobs at once, to judge ugliness and to judge politics. It does only one: it separates what answers for itself from what is mere output.

Ecology, now, for real, without the figures that make you shiver and that collapse on verification.

I'm not going to tell you "one prompt equals so many liters of water," because it's false, and *Le Monde* itself had to correct its own water figure the day it was published. The real grievance isn't there. It holds in two words from the IPCC, and it's implacable. There remains a finite carbon budget to hold to one and a half degrees. At the current rate, about six years. Six years, not a slogan, a figure from the synthesis report. The electricity demand of data centers has doubled and keeps climbing while the rest of the economy painfully tries to decarbonize. The problem with AI isn't that it pollutes in the absolute, it's that it opens a conflict of use over an almost exhausted budget, and that in certain cases it restarts fossil-fuel plants, which locks twenty to forty years of emissions into the concrete. The IPCC has a word for the solution, and it isn't a militant posture, it's a scientific category: demand-side mitigation. Deciding what to spend a scarce budget on. Frugal AI, made for a precise task, on a decarbonized grid, capped and transparent about what it consumes. No magic threshold below which it would be innocent. Just an arbitration, and when the arbitration isn't made, it isn't chance that chooses in our place, it's those who build the data centers.

And since we're talking about choice, let's talk about the one the boycott never calls into question: the State.

The op-ed asks individuals to unplug. Meanwhile the State rolls out the red carpet. Special status for data centers: the simplification law of May 26, 2026 makes the biggest of them projects of major national interest, transfers the permit to the prefect and reserves them a priority connection to the electrical grid, without demanding a single jobs figure in return. Tens of billions in investment made easy, SoftBank, the Emirates, Brookfield. And the same State, on December 18, 2025, rejected in the Senate a continuity income for artist-authors, two hundred six votes to one hundred eighteen, for a cost the Minister of Culture, Rachida Dati, estimated at eight hundred million, less than two percent of the forty-five billion firm from a single one of those funds. That's what leaves people stranded. Not AI in itself. The pairing: massive public acceleration on one side, refusal of the social safety net on the other. The op-ed targets the individual gesture of the consumer and leaves alone the only actor that really has the power to arbitrate. That's the hole in the middle of the boycott.

I'm not going to end by telling you the future will prove me right. I don't know, and those who claim to know, in one camp as in the other, are selling you the same tranquilizer. I'm going to end with a gesture, because that's what we can hold on to.

Tonight, if you make images with these machines, don't close the tab thinking you've washed your hands, and don't sign the blank check of trust either. Do something smaller and harder. Learn how your model is made. Run one you own, on your machine, in your room that heats up. Retrain it on what's yours. And know, while the card's fan rises in the silence, that while you hold it, it holds you a little too. That it runs on a chain you didn't choose. And that your workshop will be worth nothing as long as it stays alone, without the commons, without the others, without the strike. Fight for what can only be won together: a social status and unemployment insurance for artist-authors, a public compute fund and models held in common, a right and a payment on the training of our works, wrested as the screenwriters did in 2023. The gesture you make at home is the beginning, not the end. The lever is collective. It's not a victory. It's the place where we take back the helm. For now that's all I have to offer you, and I'd rather give it to you than promise you tomorrow.
